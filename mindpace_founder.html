About the founder

I didn't build this
to sell an app.
I built it to stay alive.

A personal story about bipolar disorder, breathwork, and what happens when a psychology graduate starts treating their own nervous system like a data set.

I was diagnosed with bipolar disorder at 18. That same year, I sat my first ten-day silent Vipassana retreat — not because I was spiritually inclined, but because I was desperate. I needed something that wasn't a pill.

By 24, I was a qualified yoga instructor. Not as a career pivot — as a survival strategy. The practice helped with the anxiety. It helped with the cycling moods. It gave me something concrete to come back to when everything else felt abstract.

But I also lost two friends to suicide. And those losses changed something. I stopped being interested in wellness as self-improvement. I became interested in it as a matter of life and death — for people who couldn't access therapy, who couldn't afford medication, who were just trying to make it through the day.

For a long time I thought I could think my way out of my illness. My degree is in psychology and biology. I started a project called Health Mental — trying to map out the connections between fascia, gut health, and the nervous system. I got deep into polyvagal theory. I became obsessed with the idea that the body keeps score, and that the body could also be the solution.

"I went from someone who was yelling — at others, at myself — to someone who could feel the charge building before it crested."

What I kept coming back to was breath. It's the one autonomic function you can consciously control. And when I started tracking my breathwork practice — seeing the data, watching the pattern emerge — something shifted. It wasn't that I became more disciplined. It was that I stopped needing to be.

MindPace is built for people who struggle to maintain habits. Including me.
